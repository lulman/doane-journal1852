“Whatever Occurs on Board Worthy of Note”

On Friday, February 6, 1852, sixteen-year-old Louisa Doane and her mother, Almira, set sail from New York in a merchant ship bound for Marseille, France and commanded by her father, Edmund N. Doane. It was not unusual for captains’ wives and children to accompany them on long voyages (“Families at Sea: the Captain’s Wife”; Druett 90), and judging from Louisa Doane’s familiarity with ships’ rigging, winds at sea, measurements of latitude and longitude, ships’ maneuvers, ships’ crew, and various types of sailing vessels, this trip was probably not her first. The five-month voyage to and from Marseille followed a typical north-Atlantic route from New York to southern France via the Grand Banks of Newfoundland, the Azores, the Strait of Gibraltar, and the western Mediterranean. Doane’s account of the voyage says nothing about the business purpose of the trip; rather, she focuses on her life aboard ship (playing with her pet cat, reading, sewing, cooking, cleaning, dealing with seasickness) as well as the behavior of the ship’s crew and shipboard discipline, all punctuated by accounts of weather, the ship’s progress, and sightings of other ships.

Unlike the thousands of Americans who made the trip across the Atlantic in the nineteenth century, bent on exploring fashionable European destinations (Schriber 13, Stowe 3-15 ), Louisa Doane, accompanying her parents on a commercial venture, was one of a number of women who were, as Mary Suzanne Schriber puts it, "accidental tourists" (13). After a trying 54 days at sea, Doane and her family spent just 23 days on the continent, throughout which they remained in the busy seaport of Marseille. It is uncertain, therefore, whether Doane would have looked forward to her time in Marseille in the same way that other American travelers eagerly anticipated their grand European tours. Her desire to reach Marseille, exhibited in her entry for March 29, seems not to stem from the excitement of seeing new places, but rather from an understandable longing to finally get off the ship: "if this wind continues we shall get there tomorrow morning . . . Oh dear I hope we shall, for this is fifty two days at sea which is a long passage, and I am quite tired."

Not surprisingly then, Doane's first entries upon arriving in Marseille focus on food and lodging. She carefully describes their boarding house, and provides long lists of what was served to them at mealtimes:

I will now tell you what we had for dinner, beef soup rost turkey, rost beef, boiled lamb, stew[ed] chickens, potatoes, and spinage, for desert [sic] pie, oranges, apples, pears, figs, for tea we had preserved beef. boiled ham, fresh pork. white bread. butter. olives. stew[ed] pears. frosted cake. and then the fruit as we had at dinner think. we shall like [it] very much.

The extent to which she documents these details suggest the sense of joy that must have accompanied her arrival on dry land, where food was varied and plentiful, and her accommodation was, as she notes, made of "stone"!

Later, taking advantage of medical care unavailable at sea, her father seeks treatment for gout, and she sees a dentist

to have my teeth filled, he first claned [sic] my teeth, took all the tarter [sic] off. he said I had one tooth that must be filled, and two that the nerve must be killed, they was [sic] too far gone to [be] filled, but he could kill the nerve so they would never trouble me

During their stay in Marseille, the Doanes also socialize with seafaring friends whose paths have crossed theirs in southern France, and make frequent trips around the city on foot and horseback. After nearly a month in port, the Doanes head home to New York.

A little over a month after their return, on August 5, 1852, the family departs New York again on a second voyage, this time on the ship Commonwealth, bound to Acapulco, Mexico and the Chincha Islands off the coast of Peru, sailing south along the eastern coast of South America, around Cape Horn, north to Acapulco, Mexico, back south to Callao/Lima and the Chincha Islands off the coast of Pisco, Peru, then back around Cape Horn to home, the entire voyage lasting over 14 months. For this voyage, Louisa Doane adopts somewhat broader goals for her journal. She begins by providing detailed information about the business arrangements for the voyage:

The Ship is chartered by Howland & Aspinwall to load with Coal at the Port and go to Acapulco and discharge the Coal. She is then chartered by Mr Bennet to proceed from thence to Lobes Islands [sic] on the Coast of Peru and load Guano and return to Hampden Roads [sic] for orders whether to proceed to Boston New York or Baltimore to discharge
The Ship is partly owned by Messers Foster & Nickerson is 650 Tons and is about Two years old and has good accomadation [sic] for us
My Father is to command the Ship my mother is a-going with with [sic] us my Uncle William Thacher is to go the Voyage as one of the sailors and all the others of the Ships company is Strangers
My Father and Uncle S M Nickerson has bought a Piano which cost $289, with the Stool and Cover my Father pays for one half and Uncle the other and they have set it up in the Cabin and they say I shall be welcome to the use of it on the passage out to Acapulco or to the Lobes Island [sic] and then Father is to sell it if he can and invest the procced [sic] in Hides or Horns or any thing he thinks best and bring them to New York and sell them. he agrees to not charge any freight on either the Piano or whatever he invest the proceeds in. he then agrees to divide the profit or loss on the Piano or whatever the proceeds is invested in equal with Uncle Sparrow

Doane also commits herself to a more systematic approach to record-keeping, blending her personal journal with aspects of a traditional ship’s log:

I shall now commence my Journal and endeavor to write every day at 12 oclock M and give a discription [sic] of our Voyage record the distance we run every day the changes of wind and weather and whatever occurs on board worthy of note and the time of arriving and leaving our Port of destination

Though many of these elements appear in her journal, Doane doesn’t write as regularly as she planned, and she leaves the story incomplete, stopping soon after the Commonwealth crosses the equator heading north on their voyage home. Still, Doane’s journal complements the story of life at sea aboard merchant ships in the mid-nineteenth century told in many journals from the period. In particular, she provides rich details about the merchant fleet, the guano mining that drove traffic around Cape Horn, violence and discipline aboard ship, and the risks and treatment of disease on long voyages.

“The Ship is chartered by Howland & Aspinwall”

The Commonwealth’s voyage was chartered in part by Howland and Aspinwall—pioneers of the maritime marine industry—and the partnership of Foster and Nickerson, who were partial owners of the Commonwealth. Howland and Aspinwall is credited with funding the construction of innovative streamlined ship designs such as the Sea Witch, which traveled from New York to China in a record seventy-nine days in 1849. Records set by ships such as the Sea Witch created a shipbuilding market driven by a desire for faster transportation (Delgado 4), and led to competitions such as the "Deep Sea Derby" race around Cape Horn in 1852 (the Commonwealth meets two of the ships involved in the derby, the Whirlwind and the Dauntless). Readers can sense some of the interest generated by these competitions in Louisa Doane’s frequent and flattering references to clipper ships.

Doane notes several types of merchant vessels that the Commonwealth encounters: clipper ships, schooners, brigs, barques, and packet ships under sail, as well as lighters, captain’s gig boats, and other oar-powered craft used in port. However, Doane measures other ships against clipper ships (once saying of the Berlin, “it is a fine Ship altho [sic] not a Clipper”), for she is sailing at the height of the American clipper ship era, which extended roughly from 1820 to 1870 ("The Great Clippers"). The range of clipper ship designs was so diverse that it is impossible to make easy generalizations about their appearance—the term "clipper ship" was applied to diverse ships designed to be very fast, and they often carried very valuable items (Hittel). The Commonwealth encounters several clipper ships—including the Whirlwind, Alboni, and Louisa—as they were often used in their heyday on the long Cape Horn route linking the east and west coasts of North America. Doane also records encounters with schooners, barques, and brigs, whose different sail-plans and rigging she apparently knew well enough to identify them on sight at sea. The Commonwealth also encounters several steamers, including the Winfield Scott and Golden Gate, for their voyage occurs during the long transition from sail to steam and (later) diesel.

Speed aside, the primary concern for the master of any maritime vessel was his responsibility for the ship and the ship's cargo (The American Ship-Master's Daily Assistant 1). According to maritime laws, the master of the vessel remained responsible for all damages occurring to cargo once aboard except for damages attributed to "an act of God" or theft by piracy at sea. Any damage occurring due to poor ship maintenance, theft occurring while at port, damage by the crew or sailors, and even damage by rats was deemed the legal responsibility of the captain. This responsibility shapes all aspects of life onboard, including ship maintenance, crew discipline, and record-keeping, and is reflected in Doane's repeated references to the ship's official documents (logs and manifests), her notations of weather and conditions at sea, and her documentation of port agents’ repeated embarkation and debarkation.

“The long dreaded Place”

The Commonwealth’s voyage followed a particularly storied route: Cape Horn holds a unique place in the lore and ritual of sea travel. Standing at Latitude 55 degrees 59 minutes South and Longitude 67 degrees 16 minutes West, Cape Horn is the southernmost point of South America. It marks the northern boundary of the Drake Passage, the 600-mile stretch of ocean between the rocky island and Antarctica, which is commonly considered the stormiest portion of ocean in the world. According to one contemporary description, "The winds blow almost continuously from the west, and although gales do not last so long in summer as in winter, they are more frequent and stronger. . . . In short, navigation is nowhere more difficult than in these waters” (Murphy 253). Nevertheless, in the immediate aftermath of the California gold rush, the Drake Passage became a busy shipping lane.

“Rounding the Horn”—traveling from 50 degrees South in the Atlantic to 50 degrees South in the Pacific—was and is a difficult, dangerous, and time-consuming passage. However, at the time of Doane’s journey, sailing around Cape Horn was the only way to “efficiently” move goods and people between the Atlantic and the Pacific coasts of North America. There was no naturally occurring break between North and South America from the Arctic Circle all the way to Cape Horn. The transcontinental railroad would not be completed until 1869, and the Panama Canal was not finished until 1914.

As the Commonwealth nears Cape Horn in early November on its westward trip through the Drake Passage, Doane marks their approach to “The long dreaded Place,” and, in company with several other ships, they round the Horn in rough weather on their way to deliver coal in Acapulco before loading Guano in the Chincha Islands of Peru.

“The Island is a Solid Rock about 150 High and the Guano is upon the top”

The Humboldt Current brings nutrient-rich water to the shores of Peru, attracting anchovies and other fish, which in turn attract birds like white-breasted cormorants, gray pelicans, and piqueros. The bird droppings, rich in nitrate, collected in the vast deposits of guano that were mined in the Chincha Islands for use as fertilizer. At its peak from around 1840-1880, the guano industry shipped upwards of 20 million tons, earning Peru around two billion dollars in profits. However, the heyday of the Chincha Islands guano industry did not last long. Resource depletion, wars, and the development of alternative fertilizers would lead to a sharp decline by the early decades of the twentieth century.

“out 84 days and seeing nothing but men"

As noted above, Captains' wives, as well as wives of ship's labourers like naval "boatswains, carpenters, and cooks," and even prostitutes would go to sea, and were provided with abilities to actively contribute to seafaring life (Cordingly, xi-xii), and many of their journals survive (Springer). Personal diaries are often gendered as female, and in the Victorian period, the diary enjoyed the distinction of being the "preeminent form of written expression among women" (Springer 94). Yet the sea journals written by women are somewhat of a hybrid form, as these diarists would often include, as does Louisa Doane, elements typically included in a ship's log, a type of writing "normally the province of men"—male sea captains (93). However, Louisa Doane’s participation in navigation and record-keeping is not unique, as the story of Mary Patten attests. In 1856, nineteen-year-old Mary Patten left New York with her husband to sail to San Francisco. On a previous voyage, she was "taught about the winds and tides," and her husband "showed her how to calculate the ship's position with the aid of the sextant and the chronometer, how to work out the correct course to steer, and how to keep a daily record of the ship's progress in the logbook" (Cordingly 112). When he collapsed while rounding Cape Horn, Patten, pregnant and with a crew on the verge of mutiny, was able to direct the Neptune's Car to San Francisco Bay.

Yet for all her attention to the details of navigation and ship’s rigging in her journal, Doane’s life on the Commonwealth still reflects conventional gender roles. Louisa and her mother cut out material for a new dress, oversee the making of pancakes, "current [sic] jelly," mince pies, sausages and donuts, and the washing of socks and handkerchiefs. As Haskell Springer suggests in "The Captain's Wife at Sea," "even as they broke with maritime tradition and went to sea, [captain's wives] found their lives more closely approximating the prescribed strictures of separate male and female spheres than did the women remaining ashore” (95). Doane and her mother were also, of course, far outnumbered by men aboard the Commonwealth, which no doubt resulted in feelings of isolation. Doane writes on March 30 of her excitement at seeing "two ladies on board" a passing ship, "which was quite a rarty to to [sic] us, as we have been out forty three days, and have seen nothing but sky and water, and a great deal of ruff [sic] weather." Springer identifies this "longing for other women" as a primary trope in captain's wives’ journals. Imagine the relief even further into the voyage, when the Doane women were "very much pleased to see a Lady after being out 84 days and seeing nothing but men."

“Father then put him in irons”

The class and gender hierarchies that would have structured Doane's life in the mid-nineteenth century are further complicated by the already complex power structures of a merchant ship like the Commonwealth. A woman in Doane's position would not have much contact with most of the crew but would generally be on formal speaking terms with only the "mates, steward, cook, and cabin boy" (Springer 95). Doane and her mother's dealings with the steward and mates seem civil and productive. They often work together cleaning the ship and performing tasks for the general upkeep of the vessel, like preparing food, general nursing, and preparing for storms. However, Doane also witnesses and records discord, violence, and strict discipline aboard the ship.

Captains were expected to maintain shipboard discipline. Falconer's Dictionary of the Sea enjoins them to

Shew a laudable example of honour and virtue to the officers and men, and to discountenance all dissolute, immoral, and disorderly practices, and such as are contrary to the rules of discipline and subordination, as well as to correct those who are guilty of such offences, as are punishable according to the usage of the sea. (Falconer,"Captain")

And, indeed, Louisa's father regularly enforces strict discipline: drunken sailors are sent below decks; Captain Doane is called to quell a fight between the First Mate and a sailor; having insulted the Captain with much "loud talk" after refusing to scrape barnacles from the ship's hull, the Second Mate is made to stand watch until he is willing to obey orders (a punishment which is also doled out to another sailor). And on January 3, 1853, the Second Mate flogs a sailor, despite the outcry against this practice in the decade from 1840-1850, until it was finally outlawed (Glenn, 408-409). The sailor continues with his transgressions, goes on "talking very saucy to [Captain Doane]" and was "then secured in Irons."

Louisa Doane reacts to some of the sailor's grievances with pity. When her father rather cruelly upbraids a sailor for asking for more potatoes for the sailors' beef, Doane "is most sorry for them." But food served as both a privilege and a means of disciplining sailors aboard the Commonwealth. At one point, Doane witnesses sailors being denied food for “misbehaving.” A sailor asks the captain if he thinks that the ration of potatoes given to the crew is enough for six men, and the captain responds by taking away all their potatoes as punishment for complaining about their fare. In another incident, food figures in the punishment of prisoners on board the Commonwealth:

The Prisoners are singing at a great rate and disturbing us very much [.] When Father [ordered?] them to stop which they did but gave him a very saucy reply, then Father [ordered?] the Second Mate not to give them but half a bread apiece for their suppers[.]

Such discipline seems especially cruel, especially given the important role that diet played aboard ship in preventing illnesses such as scurvy. Doane's presence onboard provides us with both a first-person account of such strict shipboard discipline a sympathetic view of the sailors' lot: "the poor sailor looke beatten [sic] and tired out and they have to be out on Deck and weathre [sic] it all and the sea is washing over us I pity them, I wish the Wind would come fair."

“a great deal of sickness”

Harsh discipline, rough weather, accidents, violence, diet, and infectious disease all played a role in the prevalence of injury and illness aboard ship, from seasickness, headache, bruises, broken bones, and “fits” to scurvy, cholera, and Panama Fever.

Doane’s journal suggests that her father was the chief medical provider on board, diagnosing and dispensing care often with the help of Doane and her mother. Captain Doane's service as primary medical practitioner aboard the Commonwealth points to the varied accessibility to and quality of medical care available to sailors in the 19th century. Naval sailors received higher quality and more consistent health care than merchant marine sailors. As one contemporary commentator argued: "Money would avail nothing to the public defence without a sufficient number of able and healthy men, which are the real resources of a state, and the true sinews of war" (Blane 151). By contrast, merchant mariners were seen as replaceable, and therefore commercial ship owners experienced less economic and moral pressure to maintain quality healthcare on board their ships. In his examination of 19th century British merchant ships, G. C. Cook highlights the perceived expendability of these sailors: "merchant seamen . . . lived a most erratic existence—often being employed (frequently by ship owners who were often unscrupulous) for a single voyage only" (225).

Doane’s account of sailors’ sickness seems to echo this situation. At one point, Doane reports that Captain Doane believes sailors are "making believe" that they are ill or "fained [sic] sick." Even when the captain acknowledges and treats a sailor’s illness, concern for the sailor’s wellbeing seems secondary to the sailor’s ability to perform his duties. Moreover, diagnosis and treatment of illnesses aboard ship were limited by the medical practice of the time. For example, Doane describes both kittens and sailors as having “fits.” James Ewell, in The Planter's and Mariner's Medical Companion, lists four kinds of fits: apoplectic, epileptic, fainting, and hysteric. Fainting and hysteric fits he assigned largely the province of women, while he views apoplectic and epileptic fits as very dangerous, writing of apoplectic fits, "In the cure of a disease, threatening such sudden fatality, remedies must be speedily employed" (114). Although Louisa Doane does not seem to consider one sailor's health in grave danger at the time of his fit, she asks her parents for medical advice because the sailor is convinced that he "was going to Die as he had the chills upon him." The Doanes' response is to take the sailor's pulse and then reassure him that it is strong, and he has little to worry about. Harold Langley, in a study of the early U.S. Navy, notes that the pulse was considered an indicator of health and that "If the pulse was slow or weak, the doctor concluded that the vascular fibers were weak or lax. If the pulse was fast, the physician deduced that the vascular fibers were hyperactive and that fever was present, for hyperactivity produced heat" (31). Doane does not identify the medicine that her parents dispense to the sailor, but it is likely that it was a purgative—either a laxative or an emetic—since purging was considered to be the first course of treatment when fevers were feared (Ewell 25), and cathartics (laxatives) and emetics were among three of the most commonly-prescribed treatments for ailments in the 19th century (Langley 31).

As we might expect, the medical care that the men received from Captain Doane differs from that received by Doane and her father on shore—they are treated by a dentist and a physician, respectively—though it is difficult to ascertain whether their treatment was any more effective. Captain Doane is treated for gout with a bread and milk poultice and an herbal bath in Marseilles; Doane receives an herbal tea for a fever. Both of their conditions are monitored closely and Doane reports that, had Captain Doane’s condition worsened, the doctor would have "administered leeches." In any case, medical treatment on land and in port was more consistent and higher quality for both ships’ passengers and sailors. Consuls were installed in foreign ports with whom trade agreements had been signed to ensure that sick American sailors received medical attention abroad (Langley 3).

Infectious diseases were of special concern because they spread easily onboard ships and spread from ship to ship and ship to shore in harbors and port cities, leading to the long-standing practice of quarantining ships upon arrival in ports. The Commonwealth encounters Scrofula, Cholera, and Panama Fever—a strain of influenza that caused fever, nausea, vomiting, and dehydration—while in port in Acapulco: on one ship, six people had already died from Panama Fever, and the Winfield Scott had lost fifteen passengers to illness. However, the danger of infection does not prevent the captains of the ships anchored at the Chincha Islands—and their families—from enjoy a seemingly daily round of visits and dinners.

As noted above, Doane’s journal ends abruptly soon after the Commonwealth “crossed the line” on its homeward leg—despite the fact that her journal still contains fourteen blank pages. Having avoided serious illness, survived a second “rounding of the Horn,” and finding good weather in warmer latitudes—on October 13 at 4º50' N, she writes “This is a pleasent day very warm”—perhaps nothing more “Occurs on Board Worthy of Note” to Louisa Doane.

Project Description

This edition belongs to a series of collaboratively produced electronic editions of previously unpublished nineteenth-century American manuscripts held in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library (RBMS) at The Ohio State University Libraries. Other editions include "Selected Entries from the Lucius Clark Smith Diaries, 30 July 1862 to 31 December 1862"; "My Dear Elizabeth," five letters written by Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to her sister Elizabeth Palmer Peabody between ca. 1837 and 1868; "Journal of a Tour to Europe," by Samuel Sullivan Cox; "Selected Letters from the Ivan S. Gilbert Collection of Stephens Family Travel Letters and Ephemera"; and "William B. Anderson's Civil War Correspondence, 1862-1864."

Each text was initially edited in connection with an undergraduate or graduate course on electronic textual editing offered by H. Lewis Ulman, Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University. The courses provided students with an opportunity to explore issues of textuality and mediation that arise when our cultural archives spread from page to screen and from library shelves to networked databases. Electronic textual editing served as each class's context for examining what happens—and envisioning what might happen—when artifacts in one medium are represented in another medium, especially with regard to the interpretive work of reading. Electronic textual editions also provided a contact zone that helped students reflect on what manuscript, print, and "born digital" artifacts can tell us about their unique properties and their relationships to one another.

While the students' work focused on providing reliable textual editions, they also explored four threads inextricably woven into the work of electronic textual editing: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts; the history, characteristics, and preservation of physical and digital texts; the editorial processes and decisions that shape particular interpretations and presentations of texts; and the mediation of electronic delivery systems.

These conceptual focuses, along with the time constraints of academic terms and the need to train students who had, typically, no prior experience in textual editing or the various technologies employed in electronic textual editing, strongly influenced the selection of texts in this series. Working with curators in the RBMS, Professor Ulman identified previously unpublished manuscript materials that were of potentially broad interest and that reflected a curatorial focus of the RBMS collection—for example, letters and personal diaries that connect private lives with larger historical contexts. Focusing on unique manuscript materials not, in all but one case, associated with other copies or later published versions also eliminated the need to compare multiple copies or states of the same work, perhaps held in other archives, an impractical task in the course of an academic term. Other practical matters also informed the choice of materials, including the possibility of scanning the source documents before the class so that students could work with both the original documents and high-resolutions facsimiles as they transcribed and encoded the source texts.

Thus, the editions in this series are documentary editions—interpretations and representations of single documents—designed to provide wider exposure and access to manuscript materials that might otherwise be overlooked, and to provide users with a variety of tools for studying those texts.

Over the course of several years following the classes in which students worked on the editions, Professor Ulman revised the introductory materials to ensure stylistic consistency and proofed the transcription and encoding. He also redesigned the structure of the editions to meet the requirements of Ohio State's institutional repository, evolving accessibility standards, and feedback from peer reviewers. However, he did not undertake a complete retranscription of the source text or wholesale revision of the students' introductory essays and annotations.

Editorial Method

Scanning. The manuscript pages were scanned as 24-bit TIFF images at 400 dots per inch (dpi), then converted to 8-bit JPEG images at 400 dpi and full quality. All edges were overscanned by at least 1/4 inch. No color correction was used at any stage of image preparation, but a scan of a photographer's 18% grey card on the same scanner produced RGB values at the center of 91/95/107 after applying a 150% Gaussian Blur in Adobe Photoshop to average color values. Thus, a properly configured monitor should provide a reasonable approximation of colors on the manuscript pages..

Transcription. Editing of the Doane journal emphasizes diplomatic transcription: abbreviations, recoverable cancelations, punctuation, spelling, underlining, additions above and below the line or in the margins, capitalization, line breaks, and page breaks have all been transcribed and encoded as they appear in the original manuscripts. The representation of those features varies depending on the view of the letter. An explanation of the presentation in each view of the letters can be read via the "About this View" link at the top of each view's page.

Markup and Annotation. XML encoding of the Doane journal follows The Text Encoding Initiative's (TEI) P5: Guidelines for Electronic Text Encoding and Interchange, as customized for the project using the TEI's ODD ("One Document Does it All") process. This editorial introduction and the three "views" of the letters were generated from the main XML file using XSL transformations to produce HTML Web pages. Several of the appendices were encoded in HTML separately. A more detailed description of the markup can be found via the "Appendices" menu, and all source files (XML, XSL, CSS, ODD, RNC) can be viewed and downloaded via the "Source Files" menu.

Introductory material (such as this editorial introduction) as well as annotated lists of people and places mentioned in the letters are encoded in the TEI header of the main XML document. Other explanatory annotations are linked directly from the text. Separate appendices provide further information about the project (e.g., acknowledgements, editors' bios, and so on). All these ancillary materials can be viewed via the menus at the top of each page.

Proofing. Students who created the original transcriptions proofread their own transcription and encoding. In preparation for peer review by NINES and preservation in The Ohio State University's Knowledge Bank, the transcriptions and encoding were once again proofread by Professor Ulman.

Peer Review. Anonymous reviewers for NINES provided peer-reviews for the editions, in response to which they were revised and then reviewed by NINES editors before being listed in the NINES database.

Production and Archival Location of Digital Assets. All of the files associated with this edition are accessed via, and archived in, The Ohio State University's institutional repository, the Knowledge Bank.
