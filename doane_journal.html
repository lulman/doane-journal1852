Introduction

Violence and Consequences On Board 19th Century Maritime Vessels

Life at sea was plagued with violence during the late 18th and 19th century, especially on ships carrying captives. Prisoners on board ships were treated with little or no civility, as clearly evidenced in Louisa Doane’s journal. On March 7th, 1852, Louisa describes an incident between Captain Doaneand his prisoners:


"The Prisoners are singing at a great rate and disturbing us very much [.] When Father [ordered] them to stop which they did but gave him a very saucy reply, then Father [ordered?] the Second Mate not to give them but half a bread apiece for their suppers[.]" (lines 2133-2138)

This quote demonstrates how the smallest indignation could result in the prisoners suffering often terrible consequences; in this example, an insolent remark deprived them of food. Those convicted of mutiny suffered even greater punishment. In a letter from Admiral Pringle to Evan Nepean, Esquire, on November 27th, 1797, T. Pringle describes in detail the repercussions of mutiny endured by those convicted. Pringle describes that mutiny threw ships into “a state of tumult,” often resulting in ship officers being sent ashore (Theal 207). When mutinous sailors were captured, they “were condemned by a Court Martial” and either sentenced to death or imprisonment (where chances of survival were slim due to unclean conditions) (Theal 208). When mutineers were sentenced to death, the execution took place only days later. Prisoners had no time to appeal their penalties.

In Records of the Cape Colony from December 1796, sailors on the Rattlesnake attempted to give an explanation for their mutinous thoughts by writing their grievances to His Majesty. Fifteen sailors describe that they suffered oppression under the hands of “young and inexperienced Officers” and that the Officers were men who “learned to Command before they had learned obedience” (Theal 174). Their list of complaints against their British officers is lengthy. First, they propose to the king that the officers “shall not harass nor oppress the subjects of Great Britain in any scandalous or Fraudulent manner,” causing readers to believe that treatment by British officers was, at the time, indecent (Theal 175). The sailors also wish to have “Two Washing Days in the Week instead of Two Hours” and wish “that sobriety be strictly observed” by the officers (Theal 175). Instead of being treated like equal servants of the British crown, sailors were heavily oppressed by officers and treated like slaves (similar to how Captain Doane treated his own ‘misbehaving’ sailors).

Towards the end of the 19th century, the US Navy abolished the flogging of sailors aboard sea vessels. This was a widely used tactic to control a ships crew along with any prisoners that may be aboard. It is likely that Captain Doane accepted this rule, as there is no evidence of Louisa Doane indicating her father did so. The withholding of parts of the prisoners meals is another sign that her father either disapproved of flogging or that he was abiding by the newly enacted law. One way the Navy was proposing to preserve the safety aboard ships was to begin utilizing Marine guards on sea-going vessels. “There can be no doubt as to the necessity of an increased and efficient Marine guard on board our ships, arising out of the abolition of flogging” (Senate 596). This quote, coming from a conversation between an Admiral and a Captain, acknowledges the need for further trained disciplinarians aboard vessels in the post-flogging era. During the time of Louisa Doane's voyage, martime laws were evolving and creating a more humane environment for sailors.

19th Century Fourth of July Celebrations at Home and Abroad

In the mid-nineteenth century, Independence Day was a very important day to Americans. In the United States, celebrations would last all day and include morning concerts, military reviews, children’s celebrations, processions, balloon ascensions, and firework displays. Whole towns would be extravagantly decorated with flags, flowers, and banners. But on a ship, those amenities would not be accessible. Instead, crew members would honor the birth of their nation by reciting speeches and odes, giving prayer, singing songs, and reading the Declaration of Independence. If food was available, lavish feasts would be prepared. In some cases, sickness at sea inhibited excessive celebration and the healthy settled for talking patriotically over dinner.

In regards to Fourth of July celebrations abroad, the extravagance and immensity of the celebrations varied depending on the time period and the country. In a New York Times news article published displays many of the countries that had celebrations that year for the Fourth of July including, Switzerland, England, France, and Germany. In each of these countries the holiday was celebrated in very similar ways to how Louisa Doane describes her celebration; there were banquets with a large array of food and drink and long lists of people who were in attendance and at the end of the night in France, “The utmost cordiality prevailed… and, amid great good feeling, the company separated at midnight with cheers for the French and American Republics”(New York Times) It seems that the American Independence Day throughout the world was celebrated in much the same way as it was in the States. In most cases it seems that when Fourth of July celebrations occurred over seas during the 19th century it was mostly celebrated by Americans that happen to be away from home at the time. But there are several instances where the celebrations were hosted by foreigners for their American friends. In another New York Times article published on there was a toast made by an Englishman, who was hosting the festivities for his American guests, to the health of the American President(New York Times). So over all it is clear that Independence Day was commonly recognized abroad and that Doane's description of her personal Fourth of July party was typical for Americans even though they were away from home.

Epidemics At Sea

Sea travel mobilized many people from various regions, allowing diseases and epidemics to spread very quickly. One method of containing illnesses was to try to quarantine ships with sick sailors and travelers once they moved into the harbor, because the harbor was a primary place for epidemics to spread from person to person and from ship to ship. In the article "The Panama Fever" published in Overland Monthly and Out West Magazine, a traveler mentions taking the substance "Quinine" before leaving their ship and going ashore to Panama in the hopes that the medication might prevent them from getting Panama Fever (553). The illness was a strain of influenza that caused fever, nausea, vomiting, and dehydration. Some people were able to overcome the illness, and others did not (Thorington 150).

Louisa Doane mentions Panama Fever during the time her ship was stationed in a harbor in South America. She mentions on her entry for that six people from a neighboring ship had already died from the fever and that the ship was quarantined, not allowing any passengers from it to go on shore, and potentially spread the disease.

In addition to Panama Fever, Cholera and Scurvy were very common on sea-faring vessels. In today's age Scurvy is only mentioned as an illness of the past. According to the London Journal of Medicine, Vol. 1, No. 2 (Feb. 1849), the author states, “It had become a matter of general belief, that Scurvy died with the last century: for, excepting in badly equipped vessels, the disease did not manifest itself” (181). Doctors have concluded that the treatment for Scurvy is nitrate. Scurvy was common among sailors because “holds, decks, and hatches, were so objectionable in a sanitary point of view… yet all suffered alike from Scurvy, simply because there was one peculiar condition common to all, namely, the want of vegetables” (189). In the days before refrigeration, only salted meats and grains could withstand perishing over time, and vegetables unfortunatly had a short shelf life, which made it hard to consume them over long sea voyages. If vessels ended up low on provisions, the conquered threat of Scurvy could become fearfully resurgent.

Cholera was also another disease that infected many sailors and passengers. The issue of The North American Review speaks about Cholera and says, “it began in maritime towns, as it had been said that Cholera does, and thence spread to the interior” (98). Since Cholera got its beginnings in maritime towns, it would be inevitable for a sailor to contract the disease. Later in that article, an eyewitness to an outbreak states, “the Cholera didn’t appear till seven days after she [the ship] had had intercourse with the shore” (105). This proves that Cholera has a gestation period, and the disease could easily overtake a vessel at sea in days. Cholera also thrives in filth, as ship surgeon Thomas Graham said in a journal, “There appears now to be no doubt but that a specific virus was brought on board by one of the soldiers, which has been called into action by the foul air from holds and crowded lower deck. The majority of the cases occurred among the soldiers who lived in the filthiest, most unventilated and overcrowded parts of the ship” (p12.v).

All three of these diseases spread quickly, and espicially among many individuals living in the close, unsanitary conditions on ships. Ship harbors were the perfect places for these illnesses spread, and those infected on the ships carried the illness to the next destination on their journey. The threat of an epidemic on board would easily thwart Lousia Doane's voyage. Doane and the crew had to be cognizant of hygiene, cleanliness, and provisions to avoid health related disasters on board. Doane and the crew on her ship completed their voyage without any major ilnesses, but her journal's mention of such is a historically relevant account of epidemics on sea voyages as a fairly common and often devistating occurance.

19th Century Merchant Vessels

Throughout her journal, Louisa Doane refers to a variety of sailing vessels that she encounters during her voyages abroad. Her references are not particularly informative - Doane generally only mentions the name and type of vessel, and even her most descriptive references are little more than remarks on the appearance of certain ships. This is perhaps understandable, as there seems to have been some debate as to what exactly constituted a ship in the 19th century. Author Arthur Thomas Quiller-Couch puts it best in The story of the sea, Volume 1, saying that, "A ship, according to Act of Parliament, is 'any fabric that is not propelled by oars.' This is how the law defines it, but any sailor will tell you that in this matter, as Mr. Bumble once remarked, 'the law is a hass'" (20). However brief, Doane's notations are nevertheless an indication of the importance of ships and the solidarity between sailing vessels in 19th-century sea trade. The casual, cursory remarks made in the journal suggest that Doane was at least vaguely familiar with the types of ships she mentions, and she indeed boarded many of these vessels and visited with the captains and their families.

Clipper Ships. The term "clipper ship" was applied to a ship to indicate that it sailed very fast. They often carried very valuable items. ("A history of the city of San Francisco) Extreme clippers sacrificed cargo capacity for speed. California clippers could transport large quantities of goods quickly. The range of clipper ship models were so diverse, that it is impossible to make generalizations about their appearance. The American clipper ship era really only extended from 1845 to 1859.("Clipper Ships")

Schooners. One very prevalent vessel during the 19th century was the schooner. According to the Oxford English dictionary online, a schooner is “A small sea-going fore-and-aft rigged vessel, originally with only two masts, but now often with three or four masts and carrying one or more topsails.” They were often used because they needed a comparatively smaller crew than other similar sized ships. Schooners were used for many things in the 19th century, but its original purpose was to transport cargo. Often, however, it was used for more devious purposes. There are accounts of Schooners being used to transport slaves("The Doom of the Slaver") and also being used as pirate vessels(in 1822 the schooner Aspray was chased down by two pirate vessels, both were schooners.)("Journal of Voyages"). Also, the Schooner Dolphin was used by the United States Navy in 1825 to track down mutineers from the whale ship Globe. The mutineers had taken the whaling ship to the Mulgrave islands, but when they went on shore loyal sailors took control of the ship and brought it back to the United States. At that time the commodore of the naval squadron in the Pacific was ordered to dispatch the schooner Dolphin to track down the mutineers and capture them so an example could be made of them("Journal of a Cruise of the United States Schooner Dolphin"). Also, according to Hunt's Yachting Magazine, “A large cutter of from 70 to 100 tons will be found a very far more expensive vessel to handle than a schooner of equal tonnage”(ROY). It was cheeper to use a schooner because, due to the way it was rigged, it took less people to handle it. From these examples, you can see that schooners were used for a variety of things in the 19th century, even though they were most commonly used as merchant vessels.

Barque. One type of ship mentioned specifically by Doane is the bark (alternatively spelled barque). In Man on the Ocean, an account of numerous ocean-going vessels and sailing history, Scottish author R. M. Ballantyne goes into great detail when describing the bark and what differentiates it from other similar ships. He writes, "The vessel next in size larger than the brig, is the barque. It does not follow, however, that its being larger constitutes it a barque. Some brigs are larger than barques, but generally the barque is the larger vessel. The difference between a barque and a brig is that the former has three masts, the two front ones being square-rigged, and the mizzen being fore-and-aft rigged" (214). Despite having very distinct sail-plans and rigging, barques were annoyingly similar to brigs and barquentines, and the varying appearance of the barque makes it even more difficult to differentiate it from other ships. For instance, adding a fore-and-aft-rigged mizzen-mast to the after part of a brig would convert it into a barque, just as a sharply-built and trim-rigged clipper barque is simply a fast-sailing barque (214). Although she never goes into such technical detail, Doane's references to ships of various types suggests two things - that she either had enough knowledge of seafaring ships to differentiate the vessels, or that she had enough interest in ships to learn such information from someone else and document it in her journal.

Honoring the Dead at Sea

Burial at sea was not the ideal way for someone to be buried in the 1800s, but a necessity when someone died while on a ship at sea. When a person was buried at sea, there was no way to mark the spot where the body was buried, which meant family and friends could not visit in the future to mourn. An editorial article in the Maryland Gazette from 1829 describes how members of a ship’s crew wrapped the dead body in a hammock and put the body in a coffin before tossing it overboard (A Burial at Sea). The crew wanted to respect the life lost by covering the body, though the body would sink to the bottom of the sea once thrown overboard. Doane mentions in her journal that the bodies of the dead were also wrapped in cloth before the burial, but since Doane was in the harbor, the crew was able to bury the body. Even though the body was buried, the crew sent out a small boat to sea in memory of the life lost. A crew would most likely not come prepared to handle a death while at sea, but both crews did their best to respect the body and keep it covered for burial.

Beyond the practical reasons for burial at sea, there was often much superstition surrounding the ceremony. On land, many burial rituals developed around the fear that the dead would return to haunt the living. Sailors struggled with this same concern, and many of their burial practices parroted land practices. The corpse on land is removed from a house feet first, so that the spirit would be unable to find its way back to its former home. Sailors adapted this practice to the sea by placing weights at the feet of the hammock so the feet would sink first, and the front of the body was usually oriented away from the ship when it was dumped into the sea (Stewart 280). Many other superstitions surrounded the burial process and the separation of dead from the living at sea, but Doane curiously does not seem to share these concerns. While she describes the sewing up of the bodies and once mentions that the deceased’s friends went to see the burial, she never mentions any ceremony surrounding the disposal of the dead, and she does not seem to share the common sailor’s fear of the soul returning if it is not properly laid to rest.

In her journal, Doane writes of deaths and funerals aboard the ship in a very professional manner. Because she is in some ways an apprentice to her father, Doane’s journal is more a captain’s log than an emotional account of events that occurred on ship. While many travel accounts from the time describe the feelings evoked by the ceremony use words like "solemn" and "impressive" to describe it, Doane refrains from describing the emotional aspects of the burial (Irving 237 )(Coultor 223). Instead, she provides a factual description of the practices, such as wrapping the bodies in cloth and sending them out in a boat. Death and funerals are a difficult part of life for many people, but visiting a burial site often helps with the feeling of loss of a loved one. However, Doane shows others(burials throughout the journal took place on other ships) who lost loved ones at sea who did not have the luxury of visiting a burial site. But Doane seems to handle her emotions very well, saying only that the sight of one dead man made her “feel sad” (Doane 47b). By reading what Doane says about death and burials at sea, we can see that she is a very strong girl who is much wiser than her young years.

Discipline and Prisoner Treatment Aboard Ship

Discipline was an important part of life aboard ship. From the captain's stand point, it was needed for order and allowed jobs on ship to be performed safely and effectively (Pollock 24). Discipline was seen as needed for the protection of the sailors. The captain of the ship was the enforcer of all punishment. Discipline on board ship was based on the captain (Hodgskin 35). If the captain of the ship changed or if a crew member joined a new ship the rules and regulations changed. Discipline was considered a direct reflection on the captain (Hodgskin 35). If a ship had a long list of crew members getting punished, it looked bad on the captain. Captain Doane would have continuingly been dealing with the discipline of his crew and prisoners. Some of the problems he would have faced with his crew and prisoners would have com from problems like drunkness, rioting, theft, and unlawful violence (Pollock 24). According to several of Lousia Doane'sentries, the crew and prisoners on her father's ship were constanttly trying to steal food. On , the prisoners were caught stealing bread. On , a sailor asked Captain Doane for more molasses. Captain Doane says the sailor had already received his ration of molasses and will be given no more. The sailor threatens to steal the molasses. Poor conditions on many ships were thought to escalate problems on board. However, a good captain only used punishment if it was absolutely necessary.

In the event that a sailor commited an offense that warranted imprisonment, it was again up to the captain to mete out punishment beyond that. For more serious crimes, they were sometimes removed from the ship completely. In her entry from , Louisa Doane describes an incident in which the prisoners are freed after rats chew through their iron shackles, and are roaming freely inside the ship. She writes that her father summoned an armed force to deal with the disobedience, and goes to shore to determine their ultimate fate. A few sentences later she says that "at noon a Policeman came off and took them all on Shore and [put] them in Prison". For lesser offenses, the punishments were more mundane on the Commonwealth. In an entry on , the prisoners are singing and being "saucy", and are rebuked by having their suppers cut down to half a piece of bread. On , she recalls an occurrence when a fighting sailor is whipped and put in irons. In the first half of the 19th century, there was a call to replace the more severe forms of corporal punishment with more humane treatment, as described by Myra C. Glenn in the article "The Naval Reform Campaign Against Flogging: A Case Study in Changing Attitudes Toward Corporal Punishment, 1830-1850". Glenn writes that the reformers "sought to discipline the mind, not to punish the body", and in naval flogging was outlawed (409). However, on a merchant ship such as the Commonwealth, not bound by the laws of the military, the ship's captain was essentially free to do as he wished to the sailors, including flogging, which at times Glenn says had the unintended effect of promoting "disorder and insubordination...[and that]repeated floggings "hardened" sailors against officers and engendered "baneful feelings of insubordination" (414). In some ways this harsh treatment of prisoners explains the continuous disciplinary problems that Captain Doane has with his sailors, as he is quite a harsh leader and disciplinarian.

Guano was of surplus in Peru for a very long time, mostly because of their fish market. Certain fish like anchovies and others were attracted to the (Humboldt Current in the Eastern Pacific ocean) which in turn attracted birds like white-breast cormorants, gray pelicans, and piqueros to the area for the fish. The Humboldt Current brings in nutrient filled water to the shores vital for the guano and sustains the (marine ecosystem for marine life and birds.) The bird droppings then would retain the nitrate in it and then producing Guano. The industry was at its peak from around 1840-1880, when they sold upwards of 20 million tons mostly to Great Britain, (earning the country of Peru around 2 billion dollars in profits.)

Guano was used for fertilizer and fire powder during this time and was in great demand. Peru then lost the rights of the guano to Chile and then the rest to France when the countries were preparing for war. The situation of the industry turned sour after World War I when the Germans invented something in place of guano which was the Haber process, making guano have no value. The fish population declined which in turn meant less and less birds that would come there, which meant less bird droppings for the guano to produce. (There is also the Guano Islands Act of 1856 where the islands that produce the guano are protected and allows U.S. civilians to take possession of these islands.)

Health and Medicine in the 19th-Century Merchant Marine

One of the topics Louisa Doane discusses in detail in her journal is that of health. Her concern in reporting on this topic reflects a broader interest in the health and medical treatment of sailors and sea-travelers evidenced in publications of the time. The issue of The Friend: A Religious and Literary Journal reprints a review of the new edition of Physician for Ships which originally appeared in the Boston Medical and Surgical Review. The reviewer remarks favorably on the volume, saying, "Sailors suffer exceedingly for the want of medical advice. All vessels, or nearly all, have a medicine chest, but no medical directions worthy of credence...Our merchants ought to put a copy on board of every vessel leaving port" (107). Doane makes no mention of such a book on board the ships she sails on, but Doane does mention her father administering medicine on several occasions. The picture Louisa Doane draws of seafaring medicine, therefore, shows her father as the chief medical man on board, diagnosing and dispensing according to his judgment, and often with the help of Doane and her mother. Significantly, the quality of care that the men receive under Captain Doane’s command stands in contrast to that received by Doane and her father on shore from a licensed physician. With some historical context, one may read Doane’s journal as evidence of how the differential care of sailors and individuals at sea versus on land highlighted class differences, and particularly demonstrates the common disregard for the health of merchant mariners in the 19th century.

Disparate Care: The Navy and the Merchant Marine Captain Doane's service as primary medical practitioner aboard the Commonwealth points to the varied accessibility to and quality of medical care available to sailors in the 19th century. Nineteenth century medical treatment and practice for sailors was differentiated by employer affiliation: naval sailors received higher quality and more consistent health care than merchant marine sailors. This was due in large part to the fact that naval sailors were responsible for protecting the nation, whereas merchant mariners were often employed on a case-by-case basis, one voyage at a time. Contemporary physician Gilbert Blane explains: "Money would avail nothing to the public defence without a sufficient number of able and healthy men, which are the real resources of a state, and the true sinews of war" (Lloyd 151). In contrast to views of naval sailors as the "true sinews of war," mercantile mariners were seen as replaceable and therefore commercial ship owners experienced less economic and moral pressure to maintain quality healthcare on board their ships. In his examination of 19th century British merchant ships, G.C. Cook highlights the perceived expendability of these sailors: "merchant seamen…lived a most erratic existence—often being employed (frequently by ship owners who were often unscrupulous) for a single voyage only" (225).

Medical Care on the Commonwealth This attitude of expendability that informed the treatment of mercantile sailors’ health is documented in Doane’s journal as well and represents a significant characterization of the relationship between Captain Doane and the sailors in his charge. The importance of their health and the seriousness of their illness is downplayed significantly in two instances, on and , 1852, respectively, wherein Doane reports that Captain Doane believes sailors are "making believe" that they are ill or "fained [sic] sick." However, even when actual illness is acknowledged and treated by Captain Doane, concern for the sailor’s wellbeing is seen second to his ability to perform the duties for which he has been hired.

A series of episodes on board the Commonwealth illustrates both the uncertainty of diagnosis and the unequal administration of medical aid. Early on in the voyage to Peru, Doane mentions her father administering treatment to a cat having a fit. In her entry Doane writes that after the administration of ether, the cat was "playing around as common." On the other hand, when a sailor has a fit Doane writes about it in her entry without mentioning any treatment given to him, merely saying "[he] soon came out of it" but noting that the next day "his eyes are very wild."

While Doane's concern for the health of her kittens during this voyage might help explain her more vague references to the treatment of the sailor, it could also be seen as reflecting the ways in which merchant sailors were not shown much concern, especially since fits were considered very serious in the 19th century. James Ewell, in The Planter's and Mariner's Medical Companion, notes that there are four kinds of fits: apoplectic, epileptic, fainting, and hysteric. Fainting and hysteric fits were largely the province of women, and Ewell discusses apoplectic and epileptic fits as being very dangerous, saying of apoplectic fits, "In the cure of a disease, threatening such sudden fatality, remedies must be speedily employed" (114).

Although Louisa Doane does not seem to consider the sailor's health as in grave danger at the time of his fit, she does note in her entry that he still seems very sick and has applied to her parents for medical advice since he is convinced that he "was going to Die as he had the chills upon him." The Doanes' response is to take the sailor's pulse and then reassure him that it is strong and he has little to worry about. Harold Langley, in a study of the early U.S. Navy, notes that the pulse was considered an indicator of health and that "If the pulse was slow or weak, the doctor concluded that the vascular fibers were weak or lax. If the pulse was fast, the physician deduced that the vascular fibers were hyperactive and that fever was present, for hyperactivity produced heat" (31). A strong pulse, therefore, would be indicative of health. The medicine which Doane says her parents dispense to the sailor is not named, but it is likely that it was a purgative—either a laxative or an emetic—since purging was considered to be the first course of treatment when fevers were feared (Ewell 25), and, as Langley notes, cathartics (laxatives) and emetics were among three of the most commonly-prescribed treatments for ailments in the 19th century (31).

Following the documentation of the sailor’s fear of Panama Fever and the administration of the medicine, Doane notes in the next day's entry that the sailor's health is greatly improved and that he has returned to his work, which illustrates that the main purpose of administering medical care to any sailor on board would be to make him fit for duty as quickly as possible. Providing medicine to sailors on board merchant vessels, as we can see from this episode, depends largely on the needs and judgment of the captain of the ship, who is invested more in maintaining their required work regimen by dosing sick sailors with medicines perceived to be generally effective than in diagnosing and treating them with care and precision.

Medical Care in Port. Despite the differential quality of medical treatment for naval and merchant seamen at sea, medical treatment on land and in port was more consistent and higher quality for both ships’ passengers and sailors. James Cassedy explains that while individual treatment for sailors varied from ship to ship, "at home...there was considerable feeling that the federal government had a responsibility to provide for seamen who were permanently disabled by shipboard accidents or who returned to the United States with serious or persistent ailments" (15-16). Consuls were installed in foreign ports with whom trade agreements had been signed to ensure that sick American sailors received medical attention abroad (Langley 3). As a result, the care that individuals received on land was significantly superior in quality to that which they received at sea.

The difference in care received by individuals at sea versus those on shore is highlighted in Doane’s journal by the fact that both she and Captain Doane are treated by a medical physican. Captain Doane is treated for gout with a bread and milk poultice and an herbal bath in Marseilles on the ; Doane receives an herbal tea for a fever on the . Both of these conditions are monitored closely and Doane reports that, had Captain Doane’s condition worsened, the doctor would have "administered leeches." It is clear from these excerpts that the well-being of Doane and her father are closely attended to and progress is observed carefully. Significantly, while Captain Doane is the primary medical practitioner at sea, in port he seeks the services of a trained doctor rather than treating himself. Doane’s and Captain Doane’s confidence in the physician’s abilities is evidenced by Doane’s report of their improved conditions upon receiving treatment.

Conclusions: Medicine and Class on the Commonwealth A cursory comparison of the Doanes' own medical treatment on land versus Captain Doane's ministrations and responses to sailors' on-ship illnesses supports historical claims about the treatment of mercantile sailors in the 19th century. James Baldwin, a contemporary surgeon who sympathizes with the sailors’ position writes: "If the lives of our seamen are considered of any importance, either in a moral or commercial point of view, some regard ought surely to be extended toward the preservation of their lives, in those climates where diseases are rapid in their progress, and too generally fatal in their termination, without the prompt aid of the Physician" (qtd. in Cassedy 15). Doane's presentation of sickness on the Commonwealth demonstrates the complexity of her position—as a young woman she empathizes with their suffering, but is also distanced from it by her position as the captain's daughter.

Food and Diet Aboard 19th Century Merchant Marine Ships

Doane frequently records what she eats and cooks at sea, and she includes stories about various livestock kept on board. These notations in her journal signal to us as contemporary readers that food and diet are central concerns for crew and passengers. In fact, Doane's account suggests that food and diet are connected to all kinds of other social interactions on board, including social hierarchies, ship discipline, hospitality between ships, and general health concerns. This preoccupation with food makes sense given that while out to sea those on board only had access to food stocked on the ship or food given to them by other ships they met at sea. With limited resources, the risk of spoiling or damage to food, and both sailors and passengers to feed, food becomes bound up with the social lives of those on board in several interesting ways.

The kinds of foods Doane eats on board seem consistent with other accounts of the foods ships generally took with them to sea. In his account of life on merchant ships, Frank Thomas Bullen writes that American ships often had the best food stocks compared to other countries (214). According to his experiences sailing in the mid to late 1800s, American ships generally kept potatoes, onions, flour, dried apples, and cranberries in addition to the usual staples of salt beef and pork, bread, rice, and pea soup (214). This variety is important because ships that carried fewer vegetables often struggled with bouts of illness, particularly scurvy. Doane mentions eating plenty of salted meats, but she also records eating pancakes and potatoes, making fruit jelly, trading fruit cake with one ship, and receiving oranges, onions, and pumpkins from another ship. She notes that they carry pigs and chickens on board to kill and eat in addition to the stocked cured meat. This variety of food is probably related to her social position on board, but it demonstrates the kinds of foods ships comfortably could sail with. Her mother's birthday celebration provides a case in point: for the dinner they feast on boiled beef, pork, potatoes, prunes, wine, lemonade, plum pudding, and pumpkin pie. Even the sailors receive wine and cake for this special celebration.

Doane's journal also shows the ways in which food could function as both a privilege or a means of disciplining the sailors. In her book Food in the United States, historian Susan Williams has chronicled the ways in which food increasingly became associated with social status during the 19th century. She writes, "For a rapidly growing American middle class, the meal structure, food served, presentation, and dining rituals all reinforced new standards of gentility" (153). On board, we can see this relationship between social status and dining in several ways. At one point, the Doane family allows the carpenter, who is not usually invited to eat with them because he is a "foreigner" of unknown origins, to eat with them in the cabin. Doane later finds out that the carpenter felt embarrassed not only to be eating with them, but also to be eating while her mother sat at the table.

On other occassions, Doane witnesses sailors being denied food for 'misbehaving'. For example, on March 3rd, one sailor asks the captain if he thinks that the ration of potatoes given to the crew is enough for six men. The captain responds by taking away all their potatoes as punishment for complaining about their fare. Sources from around the time period suggest that depriving sailors of food was not necessarily considered an appropriate means of punishing them. In his 1879 edition of The seaman's friend, R. H. Dana Jr. asserts that all crew members have a right to food, and under no occassion should the ship's master deny sailors this right (209). In fact, Dana suggests that sailors could technically win money in court if they proved they had been denied food. That we see sailors denied basic food like potatoes suggests that while at sea such rules were not always followed. But Doane, as a captain's daughter, appears to have eaten very well and had access to a variety of food and cooking supplies.

"I think we have a great deal of trouble": Gender, Class, and Hierarchies in Louisa A. Doane's Journal

Women on Board. Though Doane's position as a captain's daughter aboard the Commonwealth might seem not to fit with contemporary readers' perceptions of nineteenth-century women's roles and spaces, Doane and her mother's decision to join Edmund Doane was certainly not anomalous. Despite modern stereotypes to the contrary, life aboard nineteenth century ships was not entirely homosocial. Captains' wives, as well as wives of ship's labourers like naval "boatswains, carpenters, and cooks," and even prostitutes would go to sea, and were provided with abilities to actively contribute to seafaring life (Cordingly, xi-xii). Recent archival work published by Haskell Springer in Iron men, wooden women: gender and seafaring in the Atlantic world, 1700-1920 involved gathering information from "the journals of thirty-six women who went to sea between 1826 and 1913" (92). Drawing on work like Springer's, or Joan Druett's extensive surveys of nineteeth century women at sea allows readers of Doane's sea journal to more ably contextualize her experiences, and integrate them into a wider narrative of the seafaring woman.

Doane's journal fits many of the conventions Springer and Druett's work describe. Though most primary source documents are the writings of captain's wives, not captain's daughters, though it has been estimated that "hundreds of children were raised on board ship" (Hen Frigates 90), they still provide helpful context for a young woman's life at sea. Personal diaries are often gendered as female, and in the Victorian period, the diary genre enjoyed the distinction of being the "preeminent form of written expression among women" (Springer, 94). Yet, the female-written sea journal is also somewhat of a hybrid form, as these diarists would often include elements specific to a ship's log, a type of writing "normally the province of men," specifically male sea captains (Ibid. 93). Though we are not yet sure to what specific purpose (if any) Louisa Doane kept her journal, from noting that she included latitude and longitude coordinates, carefully cited occasions for the raising and lowering of sails, and carefully noted wind and weather conditions in her entries, we are able to conjecture that she was simultaneously in the practice of learning the craft of captaining a merchant vessel.

This sort of shipboard education was not entirely uncommon, as the stories of Mary Patten and Emma Armstrong reveal. In 1856, nineteen year-old Mary Patten left New York with her husband to sail to San Francisco. On a previous voyage, she was "taught about the winds and tides," and her husband "showed her how to calculate the ship's position with the aid of the sextant and the chronometer, how to work out the correct course to steer, and how to keep a daily record of the ship's progress in the logbook" (Cordingly, 112). When he collapsed while rounding Cape Horn (on October 25, Doane herself notes that "she has heard a great deal about its being a Dreadfull Dreadful place"), Patten, pregnant and with a crew on the verge of mutiny, was able to direct the Neptune's Car to San Francisco Bay. On the same route twenty-three years later, Armstrong "was taught to navigate...finding their position every noon" under her father's guidance, a skill which helped the fifteen year-old bring the Templar, and it's $200,000 cargo, safely into port (Druett, 89-90).

Still, despite her abnormal (at least for land-bound girls) education in seafaring, Doane seems to still prescribe to typical gender roles. Springer suggests that, at times, "even as they broke with maritime tradition and went to sea, [captain's wives] found their lives more closely approximating the prescribed strictures of separate male and female spheres than did the women remaining ashore” (95). After healing from what seems to be a rather severe case of sea sickness, Almira Doane spends much time reading. She also helps her daughter cut out material for a new dress, oversees the making of pancakes, "current jelly", mince pies, sausages and donuts, and tends to washing socks and handkerchiefs: all typically "womanly" activites for a comfortable nineteenth-century homemaker.

Though often stimulating, life aboard a merchant ship was not always pleasant, especially for young, upper-middle class women like Doane. She and her mother were still very much outnumbered by men aboard the Commonwealth, and feelings of isolation were not uncommon. Doane writes on March 30 of her excitement at seeing "two ladies on board" a passing ship, "which was quite a party to to to us, as we have been out forty three days, and  have seen nothing but sky and water, and a  great deal of ruff rough weather." Springer identifies this "longing for other women" as a primary trope in the captain's wives journals that comprise his study. Imagine the relief even further into her voyage, when the Doane women were "very much  pleased to see a Lady after being out 84  days and seeing nothing but men."

Heirarchies in the Merchant Marines.The class and gender hierarchies that would have structured Doane's life in the mid-nineteenth century are further complicated by the already complex power structures of a merchant ship like the Commonwealth. As Springer notes, a woman in Doane's position would not have much contact with most of the crew, but would generally be on formal speaking terms with only the "mates, steward, cook, and cabin boy" (95). This seems to be the case in the encoded sections of the journal, but even these class divisions did not exempt Doane from being exposed to power struggles aboard the ship. According to Falconer's, one late-eighteenth century seafarer's dictionary, the role of a captain was to


Shew a laudable example of honour and virtue to the officers and men, and to discountenance all dissolute, immoral, and disorderly practices, and such as are contrary to the rules of discipline and subordination, as well as to correct those who are guilty of such offences, as are punishable according to the usage of the sea ("Captain," Falconer's Dictionary of the Sea)

Louisa's father is called upon to enforce discipline regularly. As just a few examples, February 6 sees drunken sailors sent below decks, while on March 9, Captain Doane is called to part a fight between the First Mate and a sailor. On October 15, the Second Mate insults his Captain with much "loud talk" after refusing to scrape barnacles from the ship's hull; his punishment is to stand watch until he is willing to obey orders, a punishment which is doled out to another sailor on March 9 and 10. Doane is even directly involved in a confrontation with the cabin boy on October 14, when he is "very cross and would answer us [Doane, her mother, and the cook] up quite sharp." On January 3, 1853, the Second Mate flogs a sailor, despite the outcry against this practice in the decade from 1840-1850, when it was finally outlawed (Glenn, 408-409); regardless, the sailor continues with his transgressions, and goes on "talking very saucy to [Captain Doane]" and was "then secured in Irons"

Generally, though contemporary discourses painted sailors as full of vice, "abandoned miscreants, ripe for any mischief of villainy," Doane and her mother's dealings with the Steward and Mates seem civil and productive (Wilson, xi-xii). They often work together cleaning the ship and performing tasks for the general upkeep of the vessel, like preparing food, general nursing, and preparing for storms. Doane even reacts to some of the sailor's grievances with pity. When her father upbraids a sailor for asking for more potatoes with his beef on March 3, 1852, Doane "is most sorry for them." She learns to respect the practical hardships of seafaring for these working men during a harsh storm on November 10,


the  poor sailors lookelook beattenbeaten and tired out and they have to be out on Deck weathre weather it  all and the sea is washing over us I pity  them. I wish the Wind would come fair.

Marseille. Unlike the thousands of Americans who made the trip across the Atlantic in the nineteenth century, bent on exploring fashionable European destinations ((Scriber 13), ( Stowe 3-15 )), Louisa Doane was one of a number of women who were, as Mary Suzanne Schriber puts it, ""accidental tourists"" ((13)). Doane travelled in the capacity of daughter, accompanying her merchant father as he travelled to France for business. After a trying 54 days at sea, Doane and her family spent just 23 days on the continent, throughout which they remained in the busy sea-port city of Marseille. It is uncertain, therefore, whether Doane would have looked forward to her time in Marseille in the same way that other American travelers eagerly anticipated their grand European tours. Her desire to reach Marseille, exhibited in her entry dated 29th March, seems not to stem from the excitement of seeing new places, but rather from an understandable longing to finally get off the ship: ""if this wind continues we shall get there tomorrow morning [. . .] Oh dear I hope we shall, for this is fifty two days at sea which is a long passage, and I am quite tired." "

Not surprisingly then, Doane's first entries upon arriving in Marseille focus on food and lodging. She carefully describes their boarding house, and provides long lists of what was served to them at mealtimes:


I will now tell you what we had for dinner, beef soup rost roastturkey, rost roast beef, boiled lamb, stewed chickens, potatoes, and spinagespinach, for desert dessertpie, oranges, apples, pears, figs, for tea we had preserved beef, boiled ham, fresh pork, white bread, butter, olives, stewed pears, frosted cake, and then the fruit as we had at dinner (1 April 1852).

The extent to which she documents these details suggest the sense of joy that must have accompanied her arrival on dry land, where food was varied and plentiful, and her accomodation was, as she notes, made of ""stone" (1 April 1852)" !

During their time in Marseille, Doane and her parents chose to stay at an American hotel. Writing for the The Western Monthly in 1869, Joseph Kirkland was quick to criticize such institutions, arguing that, ""to go to an American hotel abroad, and otherwise to herd with one's own fellow countrymen, is a confession of homesickness and of regret for having left home at all. The place to see Americans is America. In foreign lands the true sight to be seen is "'These Foreigners'" " " (248). Doane, however, did not share his contempt. She notes that the landlady, though French, had happily, ""been in New York a number of years and could speak very good english" " and is delighted to find that friends of their's, a Captain Eliott and his family, are also staying at the hotel (1 April 1852). Again, Doane's warm enthusiasm seems to suggest some relief at once more finding herself amongst familiar company, and perhaps recalls an earlier occasion at sea when she wistfully observes women on-board a passing ship: ""we saw there were two ladies on board, which was quite a party to to us, as we have been out forty three days, and have seen nothing but sky and water, and a great deal of ruff rough weather" " (21 March 1852).

Whether or not Doane did think of herself as a young holiday-maker in France, her time in Marseille seems to be have been as much practically spent as it was leisurely. She records details of a number of visits to the dentist, and refers to a shopping trip, both of which, after so long at sea, must have been all too necessary. The imminence of their departure must surely have been ever-present in Doane's mind, and might be imagined to have had some influence over the way in which she spent her days in Marseille . Certainly, in addition to her more practical excursions, Doane seems to have been keen to make the most of her time abroad, and her journal entries describe trips around Marseille and the local culture. Although she rarely refers to sites that she visited by name, the details she gives suggest that she visited both the Abbaye Saint-Victor and the Porte D'Aix, in addition to making frequent trips around the city on foot and horseback.

Had she consulted the various contemporary guidebooks available to American tourists however, and whether or not she did is, of course, difficult to know, she may have been disappointed. A Satchel Guide for the Vacation Tourist, published in 1924, dedicates barely more than half a page to Marseille, while John Murray's A Handbook for Travellers in France (1867) and Norton's Hand-Book to Europe; or, How to Travel in the Old World (1860) have little in the way of praise to offer France's biggest sea-port. Indeed, writing in the Norton's Hand-Book, J. H. Siddons warns his readers that Marseille ""has all the disagreeable features of an ordinary sea-port town, and some very offensive ones which are peculiarly its own," " and advises that they ""regulate [their] arrival at Marseille that the place may be immediately quitted for Malta and Alexandria" ( (167)). " That is about the full extent of his observations about Doane's destination. Murray goes into more detail, but is hardly any more positive. The port he describes as the ""sewer of the city"" , the old town as " "scarcely worth entering" ((476))" , and his account of the artwork displayed in various council buildings is no less scathing: ""The subjects are all horrible"" , he notes, ""and the execution not good enough to compensate" ((467))" .

If Doane found any of this to be true, she gives no indication of it in her journal, and the delight with which she records the details of her dinners and suppers, her various expeditions and walks through the city, and the warmth with which she describes the friends that she met there, suggest that her stay in Marseille was, in spite of the guidebooks, quite pleasant. Perhaps she, unlike Murray, Siddons, and Rolfe, had an appreciation for the sea-port town that came with her maritime experience, or perhaps simply from having arrived after a very long 54 days at sea.

Aboard a Merchant Vessel

The voyages Louisa Doane's father, Captain Edmund Doane, undertook were primarily in the service of larger merchants and other industries. As captain of a merchant vessel, Captain Doane's daily routine involved an absolute awareness and intricate negotiation of responsibility for the temporary ownership of the ship's cargo. The American Ship-Master's Daily Assistant, an 1807 guide to maritime law, clearly suggests that the primary concern for the master of any vessel was his responsibility for the ship's cargo (The American Ship-Master's Daily Assistant 1). According to maritime laws, the master of the vessel remained responsible for all damages occurring to cargo once aboard. The only exceptions to these laws were any damages attributed to "an act of God" and theft by piracy at sea. Any damage occurring due to poor ship maintenance (water damage for example), theft occurring while at port, damage by the crew or sailors, and even damage by rats was deemed the legal responsibility of the captain. This principle of responsibility significantly shapes life onboard including issues of discipline and the emphasis Louisa Doane gives to the animal life onboard.

Although the owners of the ship were responsible for the cost of any damages needing attention that were not the fault of the captain or crew, the captain was required to pull into a port as soon as possible once damage detrimental to the ship and ship's cargo occurred. When questioned, the master of the vessel had to be able immediate response to be the case, making ship's logs extremely important. Any damage occurring while at sea was to be declared immediately upon the arrival of the vessel into port. Often, damage attributed to "acts of God" would require confirmation by multiple sources. These sources included not only official documents like the captain's log and ship manifests, but also testimonies by crew members or passengers. Not only does the captain's responsibility for the cargo explain Louisa Doane's repeated remarks about her father's concern for the ship's official documents (logs and manifests), but may also explain her meticulous notations of weather and conditions at sea throughout the journal as well as her meticulous documentation of agents embarkation and debarkation while at port in Marseilles and Acapulco.

The decade of the clipper ship domination saw an expansion of commercial interests that Louisa Doane's journal was clearly a part of. Although readers see Doane's ship stopping at various ports for different exchanges, one of the most fascinating episodes includes their trip to obtain guano from islands of the coast of Peru . The guano industry in the mid-nineteenth century marks a unique period in the development of international commercial interests, with some scholars declaring the guano industry and the 1856 "Guano Act" to be the first clear attempt by the United States to secure international commercial resources through legislation ("Chap. CLXIV.-An Act to Authorize Protection" 119). However, the guano episode in Louisa Doane's journal suggests that early in the 1850's, the market developed so quickly that a clear public knowledge of what the product was or what it was to be used for was not entirely clear.

Despite the impetus to proceed with caution to preserve precious cargo like the guano obtained from the Lobos Islands, Captain Edmund Doane's journeys were chartered by pioneers of the transportaion industry, each with particular attachments to developments in merchant vessels. The specific companies operating in the background of Doane's journeys include the merchant company Howland and Aspinwall who chartered part of their voyage, and the paternrship of Foster's and Nickerson who were partial owners of the Commonwealth. Howland and Aspinwall was a prestigious firm at the forefront of transportation developments in the mid-nineteenth century. The firm is credited with funding the construction of innovative streamlined ship designs like the Sea Witch that travelled from New York to China in a record seventy-nine days in 1849. Shipbuilding and merchant companies like the parternship created by Foster and Nickerson were not only deeply involved in this boom of shipbuilding, but were also particularly invested in the speed of ships rounding Cape Horn. Records set by ships such as the Sea Witch not only kept companies like Howland and Aspinwall at the forefront of commercial interests, but created a shipbuilding market driven by a desire for faster transportation. The partnership between Foster and Nickerson began, and continued with the desrire to charter faster ships around Cape Horn. This shared desire for faster transportation not only paved the way for what the American National Biography defined as a "decade of clipper ship domination of the American deepwater trade" (Delgado 4), but also created a competitive drive between captains, evidenced by what became known as the "Deep Sea Derby" race around Cape Horn in 1852. Although the Doanes' journey around the Cape was not solely to transport goods from New York to San Francisco like most ships involved in the race, this competitive drive can be felt in Doane's comparison of the speed and location of other ships with the Commonwealth.

Cape Horn - The long dreaded Place.


Cape Horn holds a unique place in the lore and ritual of sea travel. (“Rounding the Horn,” traveling from 50 degrees South in the Atlantic to 50 degrees South in the Pacific, was and is a difficult, dangerous, and time consuming maritime rite of passage.) Doane’s calculation and records of Latitude and Longitude allow us to travel with her on a journey that belongs to a unique time—and place.

The epoch of sailing around the Horn in search of guano was incredibly brief. The Peruvian guano trade was profitable, but short lived. At the time of Doane’s journey in 1852, sailing around Cape Horn was the only way to “efficiently” move goods and people from Atlantic to Pacific. There was no naturally occurring break between North and South America, from the Arctic Circle all the way to Cape Horn. The transcontinental railroad would not be completed until 1869, and the Panama Canal was not finished until 1914. Before the completion of the Canal and the availability of overland shipping, barks, windjammers, and other vessels sailed all the way around the tip of South America to reach the other side of North America.

Particularly in the immediate aftermath of the gold rush, the Drake Passage became a busy shipping lane. Gathering guano from the Chincha Islands, the purpose of Doane’s journey, added more traffic to the sea. Dangerous and uncomfortable as it was, merchant marines, passenger ships, and those who longed to move to California, during and after the gold rush, made the journey round the Horn.

Standing at Latitude 55 degrees 59 minutes South and Longitude 67 degrees 16 minutes West, Cape Horn is the southernmost point of South America. It marks the northern boundary of the Drake Passage, the 600 mile stretch of ocean between the rocky island and Antarctica, which is commonly considered the stormiest portion of ocean in the world.

(Dallas Murphy) reproduces ship owners’ instructions to captains in his authoritative celebration of Cape Horn, Rounding the Horn: Being the Story of Williwaws and Windjammers, Drake, Darwin, Murdered Missionaries and Naked Natives, a Deck's Eye View of Cape Horn: “The winds blow almost continuously from the west, and although gales do not last so long in summer as in winter, they are more frequent and stronger. . . . In short, navigation is nowhere more difficult than in these waters” (253)

The dangers and discomfort of the journey have been thoroughly documented. Two other journals from 1852, one by (Joseph Samson) and the other by (Lucy Kendall Herrick), also recount journeys around Cape Horn. Rounding the Horn, the vagaries of wind and weather and the amount of human misery caused by both, figures prominently in both. Doane, who made her journey in the same year, certainly acknowledges some of the hardships.

But while (Kendall) uses hyperbolic terms to describe her experience and (Samson) titled his book after that particular landmark, Doane acknowledges it with a simple, evocative phrase in her November 1 entry: “the long dreaded Place.” Her descriptions of the sailors’ efforts to keep the ship safe are matter of fact about wild water and wind. They adjust their sails before the wind, and try to cope with rolling seas. For example on Nov 8, she describes the night: “a heavy Squall came and the wind blowing a Huricane […] The wind howling through the rigging and the waves running high and washing over us continually the vessel diving into the sea very deep.”

Her even-handed account of a place that had (by then) acquired a reputation for savagery is a valuable addition to the resource of familiar papers. Cape Horn occupied a romantic place in the popular imagination of mid-nineteenth-century Americans—it was viewed as the end of the Earth. Other discourses are dominated by the discovery of “naked natives” and the deaths of missionaries who came to convert the Yahgon. Doane does not dwell on these discourses; her writing focuses on the height of the waves and the situation it creates for sailors without mittens.

Project Description

This edition belongs to a series of collaboratively produced electronic editions of previously unpublished nineteenth-century American manuscripts held in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library (RBMS) at The Ohio State University Libraries. Other editions include "Selected Entries from the Lucius Clark Smith Diaries, 30 July 1862 to 31 December 1862"; "My Dear Elizabeth," five letters written by Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to her sister Elizabeth Palmer Peabody between ca. 1837 and 1868; "Journal of a Tour to Europe," by Samuel Sullivan Cox; and "Selected Letters from the Ivan S. Gilbert Collection of Stephens Family Travel Letters and Ephemera."

Each text was initially edited in connection with an undergraduate or graduate course on electronic textual editing offered by H. Lewis Ulman, Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University. The courses provided students with an opportunity to explore issues of textuality and mediation that arise when our cultural archives spread from page to screen and from library shelves to networked databases. Electronic textual editing served as each class's context for examining what happens—and envisioning what might happen—when artifacts in one medium are represented in another medium, especially with regard to the interpretive work of reading. Electronic textual editions also provided a contact zone that helped students reflect on what manuscript, print, and "born digital" artifacts can tell us about their unique properties and their relationships to one another.

While the students' work focused on providing reliable textual editions, they also explored four threads inextricably woven into the work of electronic textual editing: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts; the history, characteristics, and preservation of physical and digital texts; the editorial processes and decisions that shape particular interpretations and presentations texts; and the mediation of electronic delivery systems.

These conceptual focuses, along with the time constraints of academic terms and the need to train students who had, typically, no prior experience in textual editing or the various technologies employed in electronic textual editing, strongly influenced the selection of texts in this series. Working with curators in the RBMS, Professor Ulman identified previously unpublished manuscript materials that were of potentially broad interest and that reflected a curatorial focus of the RBMS collection—for example, letters and personal diaries that connect private lives with larger historical contexts. Focusing on unique manuscript materials not, in all but one case, associated with other copies or later published versions also eliminated the need to compare multiple copies or states of the same work, perhaps held in other archives, an impractical task in the course of an academic term. Other practical matters also informed the choice of materials, including the possibility of scanning the source documents before the class so that students could work with both the original documents and high-resolutions facsimiles as they transcribed and encoded the source texts.

Thus, the editions in this series are documentary editions—interpretations and representations of single documents—designed to provide wider exposure and access to manuscript materials that might otherwise be overlooked, and to provide users with a variety of tools for studying those texts.

While providing a reliable textual edition is a sine qua non, the Doane edition also explores ways of telling four stories inextricably woven into our work: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts, the history of the physical documents, the editorial process that gave rise to this particular interpretation of the texts, and the mediation of our electronic delivery system.

Over the course of several years following the classes in which students worked on the editions, Professor Ulman revised the introductory materials to ensure stylistic consistency and proofed the transcription and encoding. He also redesigned the structure of the editions to meet the requirements of Ohio State's institutional repository, evolving accessibility standards, and feedback from peer reviewers. However, he did not undertake a complete retranscription of the source text or revision of the students' introductory essays and annotations.

Editorial Method

Scanning. The manuscript pages were scanned as 24-bit TIFF images at 400 dots per inch (dpi), then coverted to 8-bit JPEG images at 400 dpi and full quality. All edges were overscanned by at least 1/4 inch. No color correction was used at any stage of image preparation, but a scan of a photographer's 18% grey card on the same scanner produced RGB values at the center of 91/95/107 after applying a 150% Gaussian Blur in Adobe Photoshop to average color values. Thus, a properly configured monitor should provide a reasonable approximation of colors on the manuscript pages..

Transcription. Editing of the Doane journal emphasizes diplomatic transcription: abbreviations, recoverable cancelations, punctuation, spelling, underlining, additions above and below the line or in the margins, capitalization, line breaks, and page breaks have all been transcribed and encoded as they appear in the original manuscripts. The representation of those features varies depending on the view of the letter. An explanation of the presentation in each view of the letters can be read via the "About this View" link at the top of each view's page.

Markup and Annotation. XML encoding of the Doane journal follows The Text Encoding Initiative's (TEI) P5: Guidelines for Electronic Text Encoding and Interchange, as customized for the project using the TEI's ODD ("One Document Does it All") process. This editorial introduction and the three "views" of the letters were generated from the main XML file using XSL transformations to produce HTML Web pages. Several of the appendices were encoded in HTML separately. A more detailed description of the markup can be found via the "Appendices" menu, and all source files (XML, XSL, CSS, ODD, RNC) can be viewed and downloaded via the "Source Files" menu.

Introductory material (such as this editorial introduction) as well as annotated lists of people, places, ships, and organizations mentioned in the letters are encoded in the TEI header of the main XML document. Other explanatory annotations are linked directly from the text. Separate appendices provide further information about the project (e.g., acknowledgements, editors' bios, and so on). All these ancillary materials can be viewed via the menus at the top of each page.

Proofing. Students who created the original transcriptions proofread their own transcription and encoding. In preparation for peer review by NINES and preservation in The Ohio State University's Knowledge Bank, the transcriptions and encoding were once again proofread by Professor Ulman.

Peer Review. Anonymous reviewers for NINES provided peer-reviews for the editions, in response to which they were revised and then reviewed by NINES editors before being listed in the NINES database.
